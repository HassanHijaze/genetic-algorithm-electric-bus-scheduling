# Computational Results


**Data note:**

The evaluated instances are IVU-based and reflect real-world BVG scheduling data. The original industrial data is not included in this public repository due to confidentiality restrictions. The experiments compare the proposed Genetic Algorithm (GA) against a reference solution (RS) on nine industrial electric bus scheduling instances. 


This document summarizes the computational results reported in the master's thesis:

> **A Genetic Algorithm for Electric Bus Scheduling**

The experiments compare the proposed Genetic Algorithm (GA) against a reference solution (RS) on nine industrial electric bus scheduling instances. The evaluated instances contain realistic service trips, deadhead connections, charging slots, and vehicle-type configurations.

Two charging settings are considered:

1. **Linear charging**
2. **Non-linear charging**

The public repository does not include the industrial IVU input data or the full private experiment pipeline. Therefore, the results below are reported for documentation and portfolio purposes.

---

## Evaluation Metric

The objective is a minimization objective combining:

- fleet size,
- total duty time,
- total driven distance.

The relative gap is computed as:

```text
gap = 100 * (RS - GA) / RS
```

where:

- `RS` is the objective value of the reference solution,
- `GA` is the objective value of the Genetic Algorithm.

A positive gap means that the GA improves the reference solution.  
A zero gap means that both solutions have the same objective value.  
A negative gap means that the reference solution performs better.

### Interpretation

In the linear charging case, the GA improves the reference solution on eight out of nine instances and matches it on one instance. No instance is worse than the reference solution.

The strongest improvement occurs on **Line 6**, where the GA reduces the fleet size from 11 to 10 vehicles and improves the objective by **7.68%**.

The heterogeneous fleet instances also show clear improvements:

- **Line 8:** 3.61%
- **Line 9:** 2.61%

Overall, the linear charging results show that the GA is consistently competitive and robust across the tested instances.

### Interpretation

The non-linear charging case is more restrictive than the linear case because the charging process depends on the battery state of charge. Even under this more difficult setting, the GA improves or matches the reference solution on eight out of nine instances.

The strongest improvement again occurs on **Line 6**, where the GA reduces the fleet size from 11 to 10 vehicles and improves the objective by **7.59%**.

The only weaker result is **Line 3**, where the GA obtains a negative gap of **-5.09%**. In this case, the reference solution performs better under the non-linear charging model.

## Key Takeaways

- The GA is competitive against the reference solution in both charging settings.
- Under linear charging, the GA is never worse than the reference solution.
- Under non-linear charging, the GA improves or matches the reference solution in eight out of nine cases.
- The largest improvement occurs on **Line 6** in both charging models.
- The GA performs well on heterogeneous fleet instances, especially Lines 8 and 9.
- The results support the thesis design choice of separating time-feasible trip-chain generation from energy-feasible duty construction.
